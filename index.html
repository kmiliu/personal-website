Neural Oscillation and Social Interaction

Problem: How does ethanol alter VTA–PFC neural coordination during social interaction?

Approach: Built end-to-end pipelines for PSD, coherence, PAC, and Granger causality on LFP signals.

Result: Discovered ethanol-induced changes in theta–gamma coupling and cross-region interactions.

Your Role

  • Built end-to-end analysis pipelines from raw LFP to publication-ready figures
  • Integrated MATLAB + Brainstorm + Python workflows for seamless processing
  • Automated event-aligned processing for behavioral epochs
  • Implemented PSD, coherence, PAC, and Granger causality analyses
  • Discovered ethanol-specific effects on theta–gamma coupling during social approach

Methods & Tools

  • Signal processing: Bandpass filtering, Hilbert transform, time-frequency decomposition
  • Metrics: Power spectral density (PSD), coherence, phase–amplitude coupling (PAC), Granger causality
  • Tools: MATLAB, Brainstorm toolbox, Python (NumPy, SciPy), data visualization
  • Workflow: Event-aligned epoching, automated batch processing, reproducible pipelines

Key Findings

Integrated behavioral segmentation with neural data to examine directional connectivity and individual variability in affect-modulated circuit communication. Presented findings at Summer Symposium 2025 and Research Pathway 2026 to over 200 students.

Reproducibility

  • Data: Internal lab dataset (Melón Lab, Wesleyan University)
  • Code: Available upon request / private GitHub
  • Environment: MATLAB R2023b, Python 3.10, NumPy, SciPy

Next Steps & Reflection

This project taught me the value of building flexible, end-to-end pipelines that can handle complex experimental designs. The integration of multiple analysis methods (PSD, coherence, PAC, Granger causality) revealed patterns that no single metric could capture alone. I'm particularly interested in extending this work to understand how individual differences in circuit dynamics relate to behavioral variability.

Boolean Genetic Networks

Problem: How sensitive are genetic regulatory networks to mutations across different attractor states?

Approach: Simulated Kauffman Boolean networks with mutation sensitivity analysis optimized for HPC.

Result: Revealed attractor-dependent vulnerability patterns and optimized computational performance for large-scale simulations.

Your Role

  • Designed and implemented Boolean network simulations (Kauffman nets)
  • Developed mutation sensitivity analysis across different attractor states
  • Optimized memory usage and computational performance for HPC clusters
  • Parallelized simulations using job arrays and efficient resource allocation

Methods & Tools

  • Model: Boolean genetic regulatory networks (Kauffman networks)
  • Analysis: Attractor identification, mutation sensitivity, robustness measures
  • Optimization: Memory-efficient data structures, parallel processing, HPC job scheduling
  • Tools: Python, NumPy, HPC (Slurm), computational biology modeling

Key Findings

Different attractor states showed varying degrees of sensitivity to mutations, suggesting that network topology and current state jointly determine robustness. Some attractors were remarkably stable, while others were highly vulnerable to single-node perturbations. This has implications for understanding biological robustness and evolvability.

Reproducibility

  • Code: Python simulation framework with configurable network parameters
  • HPC: Slurm job scripts, resource allocation strategies
  • Environment: Python 3.10, NumPy, HPC cluster environment

Next Steps & Reflection

This project taught me the importance of computational efficiency in large-scale simulations. What started as a straightforward modeling question became an exercise in optimization and parallel computing. I'm interested in extending this approach to more realistic network topologies informed by empirical gene regulatory data, and exploring how network structure shapes evolutionary dynamics.

Mastering Machine

My technical strength lies in establishing an end-to-end workflow that transforms disorganized data to reproducible evidence with clear visualization. In neuroscience projects at the lab, I routinely process LFP and behavioral data from the VTA-PFC pathway, translating statistical analyses into charts and conclusions that support evidence-based arguments. Specifically for signal processing, I bridge Brainstorm and MATLAB by performing frequency/harmonic suppression and bandpass filtering, event-based epoch segmentation (e.g., social vs. non-social, EtOH vs. saline), and baseline correction, followed by PSD using Welch's method. I conduct coherence and Granger causality analyses on VTA-PFC connections, distinguishing between intra-area phase-amplitude coupling (iPAC) and inter-area phase-amplitude coupling (irPAC). Finally, a structured export of results for review and visualization in R.

I began with coursework and lab training, translating classroom statistics and signal processing knowledge into real-world data; subsequently, I participated in Neuromatch Academy's deep learning boot camp, systematically filling gaps in modeling and evaluation frameworks. In my daily research, I continuously review the literature, reproduce previous lab scripts, and refactor fragmented scripts into parameterized, modular functions (e.g., standardizing filter and epoch parameters, and unifying export fields). Combined with simple unit tests and small-sample regression checks, this ensures modifications do not break existing results. For version control, I used Git/GitHub to track changes, documented README files, and maintained runtime environments, striving for reproducibility where the same raw data and the same parameters yield the same results.

Presenting project of Statistical Approaches for Neural Oscillation and Social Behavior Analysis in the Ventral Tegmental Area in Exley Science Center, Wesleyan University, Middletown, CT

In machine learning and interpretability, I built a small closed loop from prototype to explanation using Python: I implemented MLP classification/regression prototypes with PyTorch to understand trade-offs between width, depth, and expressiveness; For text scoring interpretability, I combined Hugging Face and SHAP to run prototypes, generating "token importance distributions + confidence intervals" to pinpoint elements influencing model decisions and preliminarily screen for potential biases.

Visualization and design represent another strength. I approach scientific graphics as information design challenges, unifying color palettes and annotation hierarchies, standardizing the presentation of axes, legends, and error bars, and controlling alignment and white space between subplots using BioRender. When necessary, I use Illustrator for final refinements to ensure posters or slides remain readable at a distance.

Emotional Stability as Operational Efficiency

Through collaboration, I realized that one of my strengths lies in stabilizing my own emotions first, then calming the team when faced with pressure.

Once, while analyzing data in the lab, our group's equipment suddenly malfunctioned, causing the sampling signals to become erratic. With tight deadlines and limited samples, the atmosphere grew tense. Another student became visibly upset, insisting we restart the experiment. Though equally anxious, my initial reaction wasn't to blame anyone or panic over redoing the work. Instead, I swiftly checked potential sources of error. I cross-checked instrument connections, parameter settings, and backup data, ultimately pinpointing poor electrode contact as the culprit. After recalibration, we salvaged the data and avoided unnecessary duplication. In that moment, I understood that emotional stability itself is a form of efficiency for me and my collaborators.

A similar scenario unfolded in an AI group project in which I participated. We had planned weekly progress reports, but midway through, one member consistently failed to deliver code due to scheduling conflicts. Others began complaining about the delay. Instead of joining in the emotional blame, I spoke privately with that student to understand where he was stuck. I helped him debug the issue and proactively adjusted task assignments at the next meeting, allowing everyone to focus on their strongest areas. Ultimately, we successfully built the model, and that student later reintegrated into the team's rhythm.

I think I hold myself to high standards, often burning the midnight oil to refine code or polish experimental charts. Yet I have tried my best not to impose this self-imposed rigor as pressure on others.

I prefer to shape the team atmosphere through steadiness and patience. For me, composure isn't emotional absence, but a way to manage energy, allowing me to think clearly and act effectively even amid conflict and uncertainty, and always stand with my team members.

Arts in the Flow

When I first came to Wesleyan, I signed up for Drawing I, even though I had never done anything close to serious drawing before. I thought drawing was mostly about skill, like getting every line right, fixing mistakes, and working until the picture looked "perfect." So when our big assignment was a life-size self-portrait, I threw myself into it.

I spent hours staring at my own reflection and the huge sheet of paper in front of me, trying to capture every line of my face. Sometimes I'd erase the same section five, six times, because it didn't look exactly right. I was working so hard that I didn't even realize how tense I was, how much I was fighting with the drawing. I thought that was what "working hard" meant: keep pushing, fix every problem, and eventually it will turn out the way you want.

One day, my professor came by, looked at what I was doing, and said softly, "It's not about how much time you spend or how many details you fix. It's about the flow of the whole drawing." That stopped me.

I stepped back and looked at the page differently. I started noticing how the lines connected, how the space felt on the paper. The drawing slowly became less stiff. It felt like it had air in it now, like it was alive.

Later, in my private jazz vocal lessons, my professor told me almost the same thing about singing: "When you improvise, stop trying to construct what you think will sound good. Let the music flow out naturally." That is, I become rigid and stiff while trying to make a harmonious chorus. At first, I didn't really understand it. I still wanted to control. I tried to plan the "right" notes. But the more I forced it, the more rigid it sounded. Only when I relaxed and trusted the music did it start to feel real and natural.

This shift made me realize that striving doesn't always mean being in control. I've always had a competitive streak, and I hate leaving things in a state of "not good enough." But now I'm starting to see that sometimes I have to loosen up first, give myself space to feel and experiment, instead of constantly chasing perfection.

When Art Sharpens Scientific Vision

For me, interdisciplinarity isn't about mechanically grafting different fields of knowledge together; it's a mindset of building bridges between reason and emotion, science and art. I've always believed understanding the world shouldn't be linear; it requires both reasoning and feeling, both calculation and depiction.

In scientific research, I study neuroscience and computational models, analyzing the rhythms of brain signals and their connections to behavior. Scientific training has conditioned me to describe phenomena through logic, data, and causality; yet, I've gradually realized that science's language often falls short of fully expressing the complexities of life. Painting helps bridge this gap: in sketching, I must grasp how light and shadow sculpt space, and how forms extend beyond the paper's surface. This visual discipline sharpens my ability to discern structural relationships in scientific charts, teaching me how to perceive the "aesthetic of form" behind data.

Once, while designing a poster, I spent hours adjusting colors and layout to ensure viewers could grasp the flow of experimental logic within seconds. These approaches, perhaps not considered "traditional science," allowed me to discover a new sense of order in expression, an order that integrates different modes of thinking.

Philosophical studies similarly broadened my cross-disciplinary perspective. Reading Husserl, I was struck by how his descriptions of "structures of experience" mirrored neural representations. Studying art history revealed that formalist painters, like scientists, uncovered visual laws through experimentation. Scientific training equipped me with precision and validation skills, while art and philosophy taught me to remain open amidst uncertainty.

This interdisciplinary mindset has become central to my learning: I pursue aesthetic order within science and seek logical structure within art. For me, the meaning of knowledge lies not in compartmentalization, but in establishing a dialogue between reason and sensibility: it is how I understand the world and, ultimately, myself.
